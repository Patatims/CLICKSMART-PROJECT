What is Cybercrime?

Cybercrime includes illegal activities carried out using the internet or digital systems, from online scams to hacking and identity theft. With cyber threats evolving daily, knowing how to spot and prevent them is your first line of defense—explore this guide to stay protected.

Cybercrime in the Philippines

Cybercrime cases in the Philippines surged by 22% in 2024, with online scams, hacking, and identity theft leading the threats. Many victims never report these crimes due to lack of awareness or fear of legal complexities.

Why It Matters Now More Than Ever

In a world where almost everything is connected online, cybercrime is no longer just a threat—it’s a reality affecting millions. From financial fraud to data breaches, understanding cyber risks is essential for safeguarding yourself and your community.

Types of Cybercriminals

Before exploring cybercrimes, let’s first identify those who commit them. Click to learn more.

Hackers

Individuals who gain unauthorized access to systems for different reasons.

Black Hat Hackers – Exploit security flaws for malicious intent. Sometimes they exploit data for their personal gain.
White Hat Hackers (Ethical Hackers) – Security experts who help companies find vulnerabilities legally.
Grey Hat Hackers – Fall between black and white hats; they break security but sometimes help fix it.
Red Hat Hackers – Hackers who actively attack black hat hackers and other cybercriminals.
Blue Hat Hackers – Security testers preventing attacks. Which are hackers hired by companies to test security before an official launch
Script Kiddies – Amateur hackers who use pre-made tools to hack without real knowledge.
Insider Hackers – Employees who exploit internal systems.

Cybercriminal Organizations

Cybercrime groups that work together for large-scale digital crime.

Cyber Mafia – Organized groups committing large-scale cybercrime.
Advanced Persistent Threat (APT) Groups – State-sponsored hackers for cyber espionage.
Dark Web Syndicates – Cybercriminals trading stolen data, hacking tools, and illegal goods via the internet.

Scammers & Fraudsters

Cybercriminals who deceive people or businesses to steal money or financial data.

Phishers – Trick victims into revealing sensitive data.
Online Scammers – Fake investment and Ponzi schemes.
Romance Scammers – Pretend to be in love to steal money.
Crypto Scammers – Fake crypto investments, rug pulls.
Credit Card Fraudsters – Steal and use credit card data.
Business Email Compromise (BEC) Criminals – Impersonate executives to trick companies into wiring money.
Money Mules – Help cybercriminals launder stolen money through bank accounts.

Cyber Extortionists

Cybercriminals who demand money by threatening victims.

Ransomware Attackers – Encrypt files, demand ransom.
Doxxers – Expose private info to blackmail victims.
Fake Kidnapping Scammers – Pretend to have kidnapped someone and demand ransom.
Sextortionists – Threaten to leak explicit content.

Cyber Terrorists

Cybercriminals who attack for ideological, political, or military reasons.

State-Sponsored Hackers – Government-backed espionage.
Hacktivists – Protestors using hacking (e.g., Anonymous).
Digital Saboteurs – Attack government or corporate networks to disrupt operations.
Election Hackers – Manipulate elections with cyberattacks.

The Insider Threats

Cybercriminals who steal trade secrets, manipulate data, or sabotage businesses.

Insider Threats – Employees who leak or misuse sensitive company data.
Corporate Spies – Steal business secrets.
Whistleblower Hackers – Leak classified information.
Supply Chain Attackers – Target software suppliers to gain access to bigger systems.

Cyberbullies & Harassers

Criminals who harass, intimidate, or exploit others online.

Cyberstalkers – Track and harass victims online.
Trolls – Provoke users with offensive comments.
Revenge Porn Criminals – Share private images without consent.
Catfishers – Pretend to be someone else online to manipulate victims.

Dark Web Criminals

Cybercriminals operating in the dark web, a hidden part of the internet.

Darknet Vendors – Sell stolen data, hacking tools.
Drug & Weapon Traders – Sell illegal goods anonymously.
Human Traffickers – Use the dark web for illegal activities.

AI Cybercriminals

Cybercriminals using artificial intelligence (AI) for digital crime.

Deepfake Scammers – Use AI to create fake videos or voices for fraud.
Automated Hacking Bots – AI systems that attack multiple targets automatically.
AI-Powered Phishing – AI-generated scam messages that mimic real people.

IoT Hackers

Cybercriminals who target Internet of Things (IoT) devices.

Smart Home Hackers – Control smart locks and cameras.
Self-Driving Car Hackers – Exploit vulnerabilities in autonomous vehicles.
Botnet Creators – Use malware to hijack IoT devices.

NFT & Crypto Scammers

Cybercriminals who exploit NFTs, blockchain, and cryptocurrency platforms.

Rug Pull Scammers – Fake NFT projects that steal investor money.
Fake Crypto Exchanges – Phishing sites that steal wallet access.
Smart Contract Exploiters – Hackers who manipulate DeFi (Decentralized Finance) platforms.

However, as technology evolves, new types of cybercriminals will continue to emerge. Staying informed about their tactics is crucial in protecting yourself from digital threats.

Scams & Online Fraud

What is it?
Scammers trick victims into giving money or personal information through deception. This includes investment fraud, fake online stores, job scams, and romance scams.

Common Types of Scams in the Philippines:

  • Investment Scams (Ponzi, Pyramid Schemes) Fraudsters promise high returns with little to no risk, luring victims into investing in fake schemes. Early investors may receive small payouts to build trust, but eventually, the scam collapses, and people lose their money.
  • Online Shopping Scams Fraudulent sellers pretend to sell products online but never deliver the items or send defective ones.
  • Fake Job Offers Scammers pose as employers, promising high-paying jobs but requiring applicants to pay a "training fee" or "processing fee" upfront.
  • Lottery & Prize Scams Victims receive fake notifications claiming they have won a prize but must pay taxes or fees to claim it.
  • Romance Scams Scammers pretend to be in a romantic relationship with victims, gaining their trust and eventually asking for money.

Example Scenarios:

  • The KAPA Community Ministry International Scam stole over ₱50 billion from Filipinos by promising 30% monthly returns on investments.
  • Fake Shopee and Lazada sellers scammed thousands of buyers by advertising high-quality gadgets and sending fake or non-existent products.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Targets individuals who voluntarily send money or personal information.
  • ✔ Victims suffer financial loss directly, rather than a business being affected.
  • ✔ Scams usually manipulate personal trust, such as fake relationships or investment promises.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Verify companies & sellers before transacting.
  • ✅ Never send money to strangers online.
  • ✅ Check official sources before responding to prize notifications.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group (PNP-ACG)
  • 📌 Securities & Exchange Commission (SEC)
  • 📌 DTI (for e-commerce scams)

Financial Fraud

What is it?
Financial fraud occurs when criminals gain unauthorized access to banking details, credit card information, or e-wallet accounts to steal money.

Common Types of Financial Fraud in the Philippines:

  • Credit Card Fraud Scammers use stolen card details to make unauthorized transactions.
  • Bank Account Takeover Criminals hack into a victim's online banking account and withdraw money.
  • ATM Skimming Devices are secretly placed on ATMs to steal card details.
  • E-Wallet Fraud Scammers trick users into giving away GCash or PayMaya login credentials.

Example Scenarios:

  • A GCash phishing scam tricked victims into sharing OTP codes, allowing scammers to withdraw money from their accounts.
  • A victim’s BDO online banking account was hacked, leading to unauthorized fund transfers.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Directly affects an individual's finances.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate fraud, financial fraud targets personal banking details.
  • ✔ Criminals exploit personal digital security weaknesses.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Enable two-factor authentication (2FA) on banking apps.
  • ✅ Never share your OTP (One-Time Password) with anyone.
  • ✅ Monitor your bank transactions regularly.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 Your bank’s fraud department
  • 📌 PNP-ACG (for hacking cases)

Identity Theft

What is it?
Identity theft occurs when criminals steal personal information (name, ID, banking details) to impersonate victims and commit fraud.

Common Types of Identity Theft in the Philippines:

  • Loan Fraud Criminals use stolen identities to apply for loans in someone else’s name.
  • SIM Card Fraud Fraudsters register SIM cards under another person's identity.
  • Fake Social Media Profiles Scammers create accounts using stolen personal data to deceive others.

Example Scenarios:

  • A scammer used a stolen National ID to apply for multiple bank loans under another person’s name.
  • Victims’ passport details were leaked in a data breach, exposing them to fraud.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Criminals steal personal details to commit fraud.
  • ✔ The financial or legal consequences directly harm the individual.
  • ✔ Victims may suffer long-term consequences like legal or credit issues.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Shred personal documents before disposal.
  • ✅ Check bank statements for suspicious activity.
  • ✅ Use strong passwords for online banking & government accounts.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 NBI Cybercrime Division
  • 📌 PNP-ACG

Phishing Attacks

What is it?
Phishing is a form of cybercrime where criminals trick victims into providing personal information, login credentials, or financial details.

Common Types of Phishing Attacks in the Philippines:

  • Email Phishing Fraudulent emails pretending to be from banks, government agencies, or trusted companies.
  • Smishing (SMS Phishing) Fake messages sent via SMS, tricking users into clicking malicious links.
  • Vishing (Voice Phishing) Phone calls from scammers posing as customer service agents to steal banking details.

Example Scenarios:

  • A fake BDO email asked customers to verify their accounts, tricking them into entering their login details on a phishing website.
  • A smishing scam (SMS phishing) sent texts pretending to be from the Bureau of Internal Revenue (BIR) to steal taxpayer information.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Targets individuals through emails, texts, or fake websites.
  • ✔ The goal is to steal personal data (bank logins, passwords, ID numbers).
  • ✔ Affects personal security rather than corporate or government systems.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Never click on suspicious links from emails or text messages.
  • ✅ Verify with official customer support before providing personal details.
  • ✅ Check email senders carefully (banks use official domains).

Where to Report:

  • 📌 Banks & Financial Institutions
  • 📌 PNP-ACG

Ransomware & Malware Attacks

What is it?
Ransomware and malware are malicious software programs designed to steal data, spy on users, or lock files until a ransom is paid.

Common Types of Ransomware & Malware Attacks in the Philippines:

  • Ransomware Locks a victim’s files and demands payment for recovery.
  • Spyware Secretly monitors a victim’s online activity.
  • Trojans & Worms Malicious programs that infect computers and spread across networks.

Example Scenarios:

  • Philippine hospitals and government agencies have been hit by ransomware attacks, locking critical records.
  • Many Filipinos have downloaded fake software updates that secretly installed malware on their devices.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Targets individuals' personal devices (laptops, smartphones, home computers).
  • ✔ Unlike corporate ransomware attacks, these crimes affect personal files and finances.
  • ✔ Victims face financial loss and personal data exposure.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Don’t download software from unknown sources.
  • ✅ Use strong antivirus programs and update software regularly.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 DICT Cybersecurity Bureau
  • 📌 PNP-ACG

Hacking & Unauthorized Account Access in the Philippines

What is it?
Hacking is the unauthorized access to a person's computer, online account, or device to steal, modify, or misuse personal information.

Common Types of Hacking in the Philippines:

  • Social Media Account Hacking Cybercriminals take control of a victim’s Facebook, Instagram, or email accounts and use them for scams or blackmail.
  • Credential Stuffing Hackers use stolen usernames and passwords from data breaches to access accounts.
  • Spyware Attacks Hackers install malicious software that secretly monitors a victim’s activity.
  • Remote Access Trojans (RATs) Malicious programs give hackers complete control over a victim's device.

Example Scenarios:

  • Victims reported that Facebook accounts were hacked and used for investment scams, messaging friends to "borrow money.
  • A hacker took over a student's email account and blackmailed them for money in exchange for access.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Hackers steal or damage individual accounts (email, social media, banking).
  • ✔ Unlike corporate hacking, these attacks do not target a company’s IT system.
  • ✔ Personal privacy and security are directly compromised.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Use strong, unique passwords for every account.
  • ✅ Enable Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) on social media and banking apps.
  • ✅ Avoid logging into accounts on public computers or shared devices.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP Anti-Cybercrime Group (PNP-ACG)
  • 📌 NBI Cybercrime Division

Cyberstalking & Online Harassment

What is it?
Cyberstalking is the repeated use of the internet to harass, intimidate, or threaten a person, while online harassment includes cyberbullying, doxxing, and trolling.

Common Types of Cyberstalking in the Philippines:

  • Threatening Messages Stalkers send repeated threats through social media or email.
  • Doxxing Stalkers publicly expose personal details, such as home addresses or phone numbers, to incite harassment.
  • Impersonation & Fake Profiles Cybercriminals create fake accounts to harass or spread false information about victims.
  • Unwanted Tracking Stalkers monitor a victim’s online activity and real-time location without consent.

Example Scenarios:

  • A woman’s ex-boyfriend stalked her online, repeatedly sending threats and tracking her whereabouts through fake accounts.
  • A celebrity experienced cyber harassment when a group leaked their personal information online, leading to death threats.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ The harassment affects one individual, not an organization.
  • ✔ The intent is to cause emotional distress to the victim.
  • ✔ Personal privacy is violated, leading to mental and emotional harm.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Block & report online stalkers on social media platforms.
  • ✅ Limit the personal information you share publicly.
  • ✅ Strengthen privacy settings on all social media accounts.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP Women and Children Protection Center (WCPC) (if the victim is a woman or child)
  • 📌 PNP-ACG (for general cyber harassment cases)

Sextortion & Revenge Porn

What is it?
Sextortion happens when criminals threaten to release explicit photos or videos of a victim unless they receive money or personal favors. Revenge porn involves sharing intimate images or videos without the victim’s consent to humiliate or blackmail them.

Common Types of Sextortion in the Philippines:

  • Fake Online Lovers Scammers build relationships online and then blackmail victims with sensitive content.
  • Hacked Private Content Cybercriminals hack cloud storage or phones to steal private photos.
  • Live Video Threats Fraudsters secretly record private video calls and use them for extortion
  • Ex-Partner Revenge Former partners leak explicit content to ruin a victim's reputation.

Example Scenarios:

  • A young woman was blackmailed after sharing private photos with a fake online boyfriend.
  • A hacker stole private videos from cloud storage and demanded money from victims.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ The crime directly targets an individual’s privacy and reputation.
  • ✔ The blackmail and emotional distress affect a single victim, not a company or organization.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate cybercrimes, sextortion is based on personal relationships and trust violations.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Never share sensitive content online or with untrusted people.
  • ✅ Use strong passwords & encryption for cloud storage.
  • ✅ If threatened, report immediately and do not engage with blackmailers.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP Women & Children Protection Center (WCPC)
  • 📌 NBI Cybercrime Division

Online Shopping Fraud

What is it?
Online shopping fraud happens when scammers set up fake online stores or impersonate sellers to deceive buyers into sending money for non-existent or defective products.

Common Types of Online Shopping Fraud in the Philippines:

  • Fake Online Stores Fraudsters create websites or social media pages selling products they don’t actually have.
  • Counterfeit Product Scams Sellers advertise original brands but send cheap knock-offs.
  • Non-Delivery Scams Buyers pay, but the seller disappears without delivering the item.
  • Payment Redirection Scams Fraudsters trick buyers into making payments outside secure e-commerce platforms.

Example Scenarios:

  • Victims of the "COD iPhone Scam" paid for an iPhone but received a box of soap instead.
  • Fake Lazada and Shopee sellers tricked customers into paying for "brand-new" items but sent damaged or non-functional products.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Scammers target individual buyers, not businesses, tricking them into buying fake or non-existent products.
  • ✔ The financial loss directly affects the victim, not a company’s financial system.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate fraud, these scams exploit personal trust and consumer vulnerability.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Buy from verified sellers on trusted platforms.
  • ✅ Use cash-on-delivery (COD) whenever possible.
  • ✅ Check seller reviews & ratings before purchasing.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 DTI Fair Trade Enforcement Bureau
  • 📌 PNP-ACG (for online payment fraud cases)

Fake Social Media Profiles & Catfishing

What is it?
Catfishing is when cybercriminals create fake social media accounts to deceive people into relationships, scams, or fraud.

Common Types of Catfishing in the Philippines:

  • Romance Scams Fraudsters pose as attractive individuals to build relationships and trick victims into sending money.
  • Fake Business Profiles Scammers impersonate businesses or influencers to deceive customers.
  • Identity Theft Catfishing Criminals steal real identities to create convincing fake accounts.
  • Political Manipulation Fake accounts spread misinformation or manipulate elections.

Example Scenarios:

  • A Filipina nurse in Dubai lost ₱1 million after falling for a "US soldier" who asked for money to "travel and marry her."
  • A fake influencer account tricked people into sending money for "exclusive coaching services."

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ The deception is aimed at an individual, often for romance scams, fraud, or manipulation.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate impersonation fraud, catfishing is usually for emotional or financial exploitation of a specific person.
  • ✔ Victims suffer emotional distress and financial loss.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Do a reverse image search on profile pictures to check for stolen identities.
  • ✅ Never send money or personal details to strangers online.
  • ✅ Be skeptical of people who refuse video calls or in-person meetings.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 Facebook or Instagram Help Center (for fake profiles)
  • 📌 PNP-ACG (for fraud cases linked to catfishing)

Online Defamation & Doxxing

What is it?
Online defamation involves spreading false information about someone to damage their reputation, while doxxing refers to leaking private information online to incite harassment.

Common Types of Online Defamation in the Philippines:

  • False Accusations Fake news or rumors are spread to ruin someone’s reputation.
  • Leaked Private Conversations Personal messages are shared publicly to embarrass the victim.
  • Doxxing Private data like addresses, phone numbers, or workplace details are leaked online.
  • Edited Images & Videos Manipulated media is used to misrepresent victims.

Example Scenarios:

  • A local politician was targeted by fake news claiming they were involved in corruption.
  • A student’s home address was leaked online, leading to harassment from strangers.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ The harm is directed at an individual’s reputation.
  • ✔ Unlike business-related defamation, this type of cybercrime involves false accusations, rumors, or leaks of personal information.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Report false content to platform moderators (Facebook, Twitter, YouTube).
  • ✅ Monitor your digital footprint and remove sensitive data online.
  • ✅ Seek legal action if defamation leads to serious consequences.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP-ACG (for cyber harassment cases)
  • 📌 Department of Justice (DOJ) – for libel & cyber libel cases

Child Exploitation & Grooming

What is it?
Child exploitation happens when criminals use the internet to manipulate minors into inappropriate actions, while grooming refers to building trust with minors for abuse.

Common Types of Child Exploitation in the Philippines:

  • Online Sexual Exploitation of Children (OSEC) Criminals force minors into inappropriate acts for live-streamed abuse.
  • Grooming on Social Media Predators contact minors pretending to be friends.
  • Child Trafficking Networks Dark web groups trade child exploitation materials.

Example Scenario:

  • The Philippines is a global hotspot for OSEC, with many cases involving foreign predators paying Filipinos to exploit children online.

Why is it Personal Cybercrime?

  • ✔ The crime directly harms a minor on a personal level.
  • ✔ Predators manipulate and exploit children, making it a highly personal and psychological crime.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate cybercrimes, this involves private interactions between criminals and victims.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Monitor children’s online activity and educate them about online dangers.
  • ✅ Report suspicious messages or online predators immediately.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 PNP-WCPC (Women & Children Protection Center)
  • 📌 NBI Cybercrime Division

Cyberterrorism

What is it?
Cyberterrorism refers to the use of digital attacks to disrupt, damage, or threaten government services, critical infrastructure, or national security. This includes hacking into government databases, launching cyberattacks on utilities, or spreading fear through digital means.

Common Types of Cyberterrorism in the Philippines:

  • Defacement of Government Websites Hackers replace official web pages with propaganda or threats.
  • Cyber Attacks on Critical Infrastructure Cyberterrorists target power grids, water systems, or emergency services.
  • Disruptive Digital Propaganda Cybercriminals spread extremist content to radicalize individuals.

Example Scenarios:

  • The Philippine military reported cyber threats from foreign hacking groups trying to disrupt national security operations.
  • In 2016, the Philippine government’s “Comelec” database was hacked, exposing the personal information of 55 million registered voters.

Why is it Government & Critical Infrastructure Cybercrime?

  • ✔ Targets national security, government systems, or critical public services.
  • ✔ Unlike corporate cybercrimes, cyberterrorism affects entire countries, not private businesses.
  • ✔ Attacks are meant to cause panic, disrupt essential services, or destabilize governments.

How to Prevent It:

  • ✅ Governments must strengthen cybersecurity defenses and train staff against cyber threats.
  • ✅ Use secure, encrypted communication for sensitive information.
  • ✅ Regularly audit and upgrade government networks to detect vulnerabilities.

Where to Report:

  • 📌 Department of Information and Communications Technology (DICT) – Cybersecurity Bureau
  • 📌 National Bureau of Investigation (NBI) – Cybercrime Division
  • 📌 Armed Forces of the Philippines (AFP) Cyber Group (for national security threats)
